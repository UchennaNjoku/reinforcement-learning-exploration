Title: 
Emergent Communication Protocols for Coordination in Partially Observable Multi-Agent Pursuit
Authors: 
U. NJOKU(1) and J. CALDERON(1).
Affiliation: 
(1) Dept. of Computer Science, Bethune-Cookman Univ., 640 Dr. Mary McLeod
Bethune Blvd., Daytona Beach, FL 32114.
Text:
Cooperative multi-agent pursuit tasks provide a visually intuitive framework for studying
coordination, wherein agents must jointly surround and intercept a moving target. These
environments present significant challenges when fields of view are limited and environment
dynamics are unpredictable. While centralized training with decentralized execution enables
effective policy learning, robust team behaviors often depend on information exchange that
exceeds what can be achieved through implicit coordination from physical observation alone.
This study investigates the autonomous emergence of communication protocols in a
multi-agent pursuit domain without hand-engineered signaling conventions. Specifically, this
research (1) evaluates the impact of learned communication on pursuit efficiency relative to
non-communicative baselines and (2) analyzes semantic associations between discrete
symbols and agents’ spatial intentions to interpret the “language” developed by the team.
We simulated these interactions in a grid-world environment using reinforcement learning
algorithms with differentiable communication channels.
Results indicated the spontaneous development of grounded symbol systems, where
clusters of messages aligned with spatial configurations and tactical intent.
Communication-enabled agents exhibited more complementary path planning, more
consistent coverage of escape routes, and clearer implicit role allocation during containment
and interception, particularly under ambiguous observations. Overall, these findings indicate
that emergent communication enables adaptive coordination without manual protocol
engineering, offering a scalable framework for multi-agent teams operating in
bandwidth-constrained or novel environments.
Keywords: multi-agent reinforcement learning, emergent communication, cooperative
pursuit, differentiable communication, partial observability, interpretability, decentralized
coordination, grid-world environments, multi-agent coordination
